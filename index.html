01 — The Struggle

What We're Fighting

After successfully forcing the government to abandon a new terminal at CDG, environmental associations and residents' collectives now face a more fragmented battle: ADP (Aéroports de Paris) is pushing forward with "smaller" infrastructure investments that collectively represent the same expansion under a different name.

New boarding halls with capacity for 4–8 million additional passengers. A new freight hub. And a corporate roadmap that openly targets 344 extra flights per day by 2050 — without any public debate on climate impact, noise, or air quality for the 3.6 million people living under the flight paths.

The core problem: Incremental expansion is harder to fight than a single mega-project. ADP is betting that breaking it into pieces will break the coalition too.
02 — Field Research

Interviews

Sophie L., 52 — Resident, Goussainville (under CDG flight path since 1970)
"We stopped them once. But they don't give up — they just repackage it. Now it's 'boarding halls', not a 'terminal.' The word changes, the concrete stays the same. My grandchildren will inherit this noise if we lose."
Sophie has been a member of the ADVOCNAR residents' collective for 11 years. Her house is directly under the runway 3 approach path. Average noise exposure: 72 dB Lden.
Karim B., 38 — Environmental lawyer, specialized in urban planning law
"The legal angle is real. ADP is required to conduct a public utility declaration for infrastructure over a certain threshold. The question is whether the sum of these 'smaller' projects triggers that threshold. If we can prove they're a single project presented as fragments, we have a case."
Karim consults for multiple associations including Réseau Action Climat. He flagged "environmental fragmentation" as a documented industry tactic to avoid cumulative impact assessments.

Key Insight from Research

Both interviews pointed to the same strategic vulnerability on ADP's side: the public doesn't see the connection between individual infrastructure projects and the cumulative 2050 target. The 344 flights/day figure is buried in corporate roadmaps. The strategy must make that connection undeniable and visceral.

03 — The Problematic

Fragmentation as a Weapon

We are not fighting a terminal. We are fighting a logic: that growth is inevitable, that infrastructure can be approved piece by piece without ever accounting for the whole, and that the people who live closest to the noise have the least power over the decision.

Our problematic: How do we make the cumulative, invisible impact of airport expansion legible — and outrageable — to people who don't yet feel personally affected?

The moment someone in Paris's 10th understands that their nephew in Sarcelles sleeps with 72 dB of engine noise, and that ADP's 2050 plan adds 344 more flights over that same house — that's when coalitions grow.
04 — The Strategy

Three-Phase Campaign

1
Make the Invisible Visible — Months 1–3
Launch the GROUND CDG digital tool (below). Door-to-door in affected communes (Goussainville, Sarcelles, Villeneuve-le-Roi, Orly zone) to gather real noise testimonies mapped to postcodes. Publish an open data map of projected noise corridors under the 2050 ADP plan. Goal: 10,000 personalized impact calculations shared on social media.
2
Build the Coalition — Months 3–6
Connect residents' collectives with climate groups (Extinction Rebellion, ANV-COP21), legal teams, and local elected officials. Present the legal fragmentation argument to sympathetic MPs. Organize a "Night of 344 Flights" — a real-time public sound installation in Paris where speakers play CDG noise for the exact duration of the extra daily flights (calculated at average 2hr per flight = 688 hours of noise).
3
Legal + Political Pressure — Months 6–18
File a legal challenge demanding a cumulative environmental impact assessment for all planned ADP investments under France's Code de l'urbanisme. Deliver the petition (below) to the Ministère de la Transition Écologique and the Autorité environnementale. Push for inclusion of CDG expansion in France's national "zones à faibles émissions" (ZFE) debate.
05 — Digital Innovation

06 — The Petition

Formal Demand

PETITION — For a Cumulative Environmental Assessment of CDG Expansion

To: The Minister of Ecological Transition, the Autorité environnementale, and the Board of Aéroports de Paris (ADP)

We, the undersigned residents, environmental organizations, and citizens, call for an immediate halt to all infrastructure investment decisions at Roissy-Charles de Gaulle Airport pending a full, transparent, and independent cumulative environmental impact assessment.

ADP's current strategy — approving new boarding halls, freight infrastructure, and capacity increases through separate administrative procedures — bypasses the spirit of France's environmental law. These projects are not independent: they serve a single corporate objective of +19% traffic growth by 2050, which means 344 additional flights over our communities every single day.

We have already succeeded in stopping the new Terminal 4. We will not accept the same expansion delivered in fragments.

```

Our Demands

  • A legally binding cumulative environmental impact assessment covering all planned CDG infrastructure investments and their 2050 traffic targets, conducted by an independent body.
  • A public inquiry (enquête publique) specifically addressing noise pollution, air quality, and public health for all communes within the 65 dB Lden isophone.
  • Alignment of ADP's infrastructure roadmap with France's obligations under the Paris Agreement and the national low-emission zone framework.
  • No construction permits issued for new CDG boarding halls or freight terminals until the above assessments are complete and publicly debated.
  • Binding caps on annual flight movements at CDG, rather than growth targets, as the baseline for any future planning permission.

The sky is not a corporate resource. The health of 3.6 million people living under these flight paths is not a line in an infrastructure budget.

```
07 — Digital Tactics

Beyond the Calculator

1. The Cumulative Map

An open-source, embeddable web map showing every planned ADP infrastructure project overlaid with noise isophone projections, air quality models, and school/hospital locations. Built on OpenStreetMap + public ADP filings. Updated as new permits are filed.

2. The 344 Alarm

A browser notification or widget that sends one alert for each "extra flight" in real time throughout the day — 344 notifications, calibrated to actual ADP flight scheduling data. It makes the abstract number experiential. You can't ignore 344 interruptions in a day.

3. ADP Shareholder Proxy Tool

France's Caisse des Dépôts holds 33% of ADP. A tool that generates a pre-written shareholder concern letter addressed to CDC for any citizen to sign and send directly — applying pressure through ADP's own ownership structure, not just the streets.

4. Sound Archive

Crowdsourced noise recordings from residents, tagged with GPS coordinates and dB levels, forming a legal-quality sonic archive. Submitted as supporting evidence in the cumulative impact assessment demand. Testimony + data.
