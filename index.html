01 — The Struggle
What We're Fighting

After successfully forcing the government to abandon Terminal 4 at CDG, environmental associations and residents' collectives now face a harder battle. ADP is pushing a €3.5–4.5 billion infrastructure programme through a voluntary "public concertation" — new boarding halls for 7–8M passengers, extensions to existing terminals, intermodal rail connections — rebranded as "anti-gigantism" and "pragmatic growth."

344 additional flights per day by 2050. The concertation, activists argue, is communication, not democracy — and no serious independent public debate has taken place.

The strategic problem: By rebranding expansion as modernisation and running a voluntary (not legally mandated) concertation, ADP avoids the independent environmental scrutiny that a full public inquiry would require. The coalition of Rester sur Terre, ADVOCNAR, and Non au Terminal 4 says the logic is the same as Terminal 4 — only the packaging changed.

03 — The Problematic
Fragmentation
as a Weapon

We are not fighting a terminal. We are fighting a logic: that growth is inevitable, that infrastructure can be approved piece by piece without accounting for the whole, and that the people living closest to the noise have the least power over the decision.

Our problematic: How do we make the cumulative, invisible impact of airport expansion legible — and outrageable — to people who don't yet feel personally affected?
04 — Campaign Strategy
Three Phases
1
Make the Invisible Visible — Months 1–3
Launch the GROUND CDG impact calculator. Door-to-door in affected communes (Goussainville, Sarcelles, Villeneuve-le-Roi) to gather noise testimonies mapped to postcodes. Coordinate with existing petition by Rester sur Terre, ADVOCNAR, and Non au Terminal 4. Goal: 10,000 personalized impact cards shared on social media.
2
Build the Coalition — Months 3–6
Connect residents' collectives with climate groups (XR, ANV-COP21), legal teams, and local MPs. Organize a "Night of 344 Flights" — a real-time sound installation in Paris where speakers replay CDG noise for the duration of the extra daily flights. Present the legal fragmentation argument to sympathetic parliamentarians.
3
Legal + Political Pressure — Months 6–18
File a legal challenge demanding a cumulative environmental impact assessment for all planned ADP investments. Deliver the petition to the Ministère de la Transition Écologique and the Autorité environnementale. Push for CDG expansion to be included in France's ZFE national debate.

06 — The Petition
Formal Demand
For a Cumulative Environmental Assessment of CDG Expansion

To: The Minister of Ecological Transition, the Autorité environnementale, and the Board of Aéroports de Paris

We, the undersigned residents, environmental organizations, and citizens, call for an immediate halt to all infrastructure investment decisions at Roissy-CDG pending a full, transparent, and independent cumulative environmental impact assessment.

ADP's "voluntary concertation" — run by the company itself — is not a legally mandated public inquiry. It produced no binding commitments and was denounced by Rester sur Terre, ADVOCNAR, and Non au Terminal 4 as a communication exercise, not a democratic process. Meanwhile, the core objective remains: +19% traffic growth by 2050, meaning 344 additional flights over our communities every day.

We stopped Terminal 4. We will not accept the same expansion delivered in fragments.

  • A legally binding cumulative environmental impact assessment covering all planned CDG infrastructure investments and their 2050 traffic targets, conducted by an independent body.
  • A public inquiry addressing noise pollution, air quality, and public health for all communes within the 65 dB Lden isophone.
  • Alignment of ADP's roadmap with France's obligations under the Paris Agreement and the national ZFE framework.
  • No construction permits for new boarding halls or terminal extensions until an independent environmental assessment is complete and publicly debated.
  • Binding caps on annual flight movements at CDG — not growth targets — as the baseline for any future planning permission.

The sky is not a corporate resource. The health of 3.6 million people under these flight paths is not a line in an infrastructure budget.
